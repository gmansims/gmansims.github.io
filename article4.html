The Ritual of Practice

Dec 30, 2025 · 7 min read

Before tournament week, professional golfers spend hours on the practice range. Not playing for score. Not competing. Just hitting balls, refining their swing, building muscle memory.

This practice isn't glamorous. There's no gallery watching. No leaderboard. No prize money. But without it, championship golf would be impossible.

Practice vs. Performance

In work, we often blur the line between practice and performance. Every commit is production code. Every design is for a real project. Every decision has immediate consequences.

This creates two problems:

  1. We never get better because we're always performing
  2. Performance suffers because we haven't practiced enough

Professionals in any field separate these modes. Musicians practice scales. Athletes drill fundamentals. Writers do morning pages. The practice isn't the performance—it makes the performance possible.

What Practice Looks Like for Builders

For people who build things—code, designs, systems, products—practice might include:

  • Coding katas or small exercises focused on technique
  • Rebuilding familiar projects with new approaches
  • Studying and recreating excellent work from others
  • Writing throwaway prototypes to explore ideas
  • Refactoring old code for the learning, not the outcome
  • Reading documentation and implementing examples

The key characteristic: the work itself is the goal, not what the work produces.

Building a Practice Ritual

A ritual has structure. It's not "practice when I feel like it." It's a repeatable routine that signals to your brain: this is practice time.

Here's a simple framework:

1. Fixed Time and Duration

Morning works well—30-60 minutes before "real" work starts. But any consistent time works. The duration should be short enough to sustain daily, long enough to get into flow.

2. Focused Scope

Don't practice everything. Pick one thing: algorithm practice, design patterns, test-driven development, performance optimization, documentation writing.

Focus on that thing for a month. Then move to the next thing. Deep practice beats scattered effort.

3. Low Stakes

Practice should feel safe. No one sees it. No one judges it. It doesn't go to production. This psychological safety is what allows real learning.

Create a "practice" folder. Use a separate repository. Work in a private notebook. Make it clear: this is the range, not the course.

4. Immediate Feedback

Good practice includes feedback loops. Tests that pass or fail. Code that runs or errors. Metrics that improve or don't.

Without feedback, practice is just motion, not progress.

What to Practice

Your practice should focus on fundamentals and weak spots:

  • Fundamentals: The basics that support everything else (clean code, testing, documentation, problem decomposition)
  • Weak spots: Skills you avoid because they're uncomfortable (performance optimization, accessibility, security, public speaking)
  • New techniques: Tools and approaches you want to add to your repertoire

When Practice Feels Like a Luxury

During busy seasons, practice time feels impossible. There's too much real work. Too many deadlines. Too much pressure.

This is exactly when professionals practice most. Tiger Woods didn't skip the range during major championship weeks. He hit more balls, not fewer.

When you're busy:

  • Practice can be 15 minutes instead of 60
  • Practice can be deliberate work on today's actual problems
  • Practice can be reviewing what you built and asking how to do it better

The ritual survives by adapting, not by being rigid.

The Long Game

Practice doesn't show results today. That's the point.

A golfer practicing putting for an hour won't see improvement in that hour. But after a month, the improvement is undeniable. After a year, it's transformative.

The same timeline applies to your craft. Daily practice feels small. Six months of daily practice changes everything.

Build the ritual. Trust the process. Show up to the range.
